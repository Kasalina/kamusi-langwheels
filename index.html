What are Language Wheels?

A Language Wheel is a colorful graphic to easily identify a language, so you can select it on a website or telephone. Each Wheel is a circle divided into 6 segments, and each segment can be one of 9 colors - resulting in 531,441 possible unique combinations. Colors have been custom-selected for all languages spoken by more than one million people and all languages with an official status in any country. For other languages, colors were initially assigned at random; people associated with each language community are eagerly invited to propose better schemes based on locally-appropriate colors through the end of 2017.

What problems do Language Wheels solve?

Choosing a language on a website or phone can be hard. If you want to read a site in German, do you choose "German", or "Deutsch", or "de", or a picture of the German flag? If you are in Switzerland, where the major languages are German, French, and Italian, you will certainly object to having your language indicated by the flag of Germany, France, or Italy! If you land on a site in another language, do you know the magic word in that language that will get you back to your own language - for example, how would you get to the German version of a site in Japanese? Not even English is easy - should it be identified by the flag of Britain where it was born or the American flag where it has the most native speakers, and what about the prevalence of English in Australia, Canada, or South Africa? What if a language has different variations, such as Canadian French versus the French of France?

A Swiss site with 3 languages is already tough. Choosing among 19 languages, such as WordReference.com, leads to some very complicated menus. A list of 46 languages, like Global Voices (visit them!) gets really messy. And, as technology becomes ever more ubiquitous, a digital presence could emerge for any of more than 7000 languages.

Language Wheels solve these problems by offering a consistent and unique visual identifier for each language, and even for each known language variety. Just as the French, Dutch, and Russians learn to identify and distinguish their national flags, people can quickly learn Wheels for the languages that interest them. Then, whenever they see their Wheel, they know they've found their language.

This site makes it easy for web and app developers to download and install the Wheels they need, using vector graphics that scale to any size. Language selection menus with embedded Wheels are also available.

How are colors decided?

At Kamusi, we believe that all languages are equal, but in digital media some are more equal than others. We began by randomly generating colors for each language with an ISO 639-3 code, as well as identified sub-varieties. We tamed the randomness with visual heuristics, for example assigning the same color to two contiguous segments as often as possible, or alternatively having the same color in directly opposite segments.

We reversed this egalitarian approach for languages with a large digital presence, any official legal status, or more than a million speakers. High numbers of people seek services in those languages, with the most-digitized languages being the most important to identify for the most people. Mega-languages like English, Chinese, Spanish, and Arabic should be identifiable at a glance. Below are the wheels for English, Chinese, Spanish, and Arabic, respectively:

When selecting colors for customization, we began by considering whether there was a close association with a national flag. However, flags were problematic, with at least 13 languages married to red, white, and blue! We tweaked the Wheels to use those colors in distinct patterns for English, French, Russian, and others. For some languages, though, we looked to other colors of significance to their speakers - for example, we ditched the red-white-blue Dutch flag in favor of the orange and white of their national football uniform.

For the 11 official languages of South Africa, we started with variations on the colors of that country's flag. We reduced the possibility for confusion, though, by using the flags of neighboring countries for languages widely spoken elsewhere, such as the colors of Botswana's flag for the Setswana language. We selected the Flag of Cusco as the basis for the vibrant Quechua Wheel, and made many similar judgements for several hundred languages.

Guidelines for submitting a wheel:

  • Use colors that have meaning locally.
  • For a set of languages that are related, consider having a consistent scheme on one part of the Wheel, and varying the colors on the other part. Don't go overboard on consistency, however - for example, it would be impossible to distinguish the 800 languages of Papua New Guinea if they were all based on the black-white-red-orange of that country's flag.
  • Arrange colors in memorable patterns, such as making one color repeat next to itself or across from itself.
  • Check the "Similar Wheels" tab to make sure your scheme, or something too much like it, is not already being used.
  • In the "Rationale" space, explain why the Wheel represents the language, in at most a few sentences.

Our Goal: An International Standard

Wheels are a simple solution to a problem for any digital service that operates in more than one language. Until now, each developer has had to tackle the problem afresh - reinventing the wheel on every website, so to speak. Were the software industry to have a systematic way of representing languages graphically, a lot of hassle would be saved in supporting multilingual services. Meanwhile, consumers would benefit from a consistent experience that made it quick and easy to identify services in their own languages. After the Wheel color schemes have been finalized with the input of interested communities, we will prepare a proposal for the International Standards Organization to adopt these icons as a global standard. The next meeting of ISO/TC37, the technical committee responsible for approving terminology and other language and content resources, will occur in June 2018. Our hope is to have a proposal ready for that meeting, although this timeframe might be optimistic. And, if the graphics become a truly popular visual mechanism for referencing language names, perhaps they could become part of the UNICODE character set, like the emoji set that now makes all national flags easily accessible as standard characters across operating systems.

Caveats and Limitations

  • The languages available are those that have been assigned ISO 639-3 codes by SIL on behalf of the International Standards Organization. In special cases, we have provisionally added a language that does not have an ISO code, but perfecting the list of the world's languages is outside of the scope of this project - please submit change requests to SIL using their formal procedure.
  • Most wheels point to a language writ large, without differentiating elements such as multiple writing systems or multiple orthographies for the same language. Please alert us if a language comes in many flavors, and we might be able to add a provisional additional wheel.
  • The language names in our configuration tool are presented in as many languages as we have data for. Ethnologue lists endonyms on each language's individual page, but we don't have the resources to scrape that data. If you can help us collect the local names for some chunk of languages, we will be thrilled to add them to our language selector tool.
  • Because Language Wheels are based on color, they inherently fail to meet Web Content Accessibility Guidelines 1.4.1 (WCAG 2.0) for the color blind.
